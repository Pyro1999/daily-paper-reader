## Abstract 9

Neural decoding is a widely-used machine learning technique for investigating how behavior, perception 10 

and cognition are represented in neural activity. However without careful application data leakage can 11 

occur, where information from the test set contaminates the training set, leading to biased estimates of 12 

decoding performance and potentially invalidating biological conclusions. Here we show that leakage 13 

can be introduced in neural decoding studies by common supervised and unsupervised preprocessing 14 

steps, including dimensionality reduction. We reveal that in some cases leakage can paradoxically 15 

decrease decoding performance relative to unbiased estimates, and we provide theoretical analyses 16 

explaining how this occurs. We also show that, for autocorrelated neural time series, randomized k-fold 17 

cross-validation can dramatically overstate decoding performance. Based on these findings, we provide 18 

detailed recommendations for avoiding data leakage in neural decoding. 19 

## 1 Introduction 20 

Neural decoding is an important tool for understanding how neural activity relates to behavior, cognition, 21 

and perception (Horikawa et al., 2013; Anumanchipalli et al., 2019; Glaser et al., 2020; Stringer 22 

et al., 2021). It is also widely used for neural engineering applications, including visual decoding and 23 

reconstruction from brain activity (Horikawa & Kamitani, 2017; Wen et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2023a,b) 24 

and brain-computer interfaces for communication, movement control, and sensory feedback (Wolpaw & 25 

McFarland, 2004; Birbaumer & Cohen, 2007; He et al., 2020; Flesher et al., 2021). Recent advances in 26 

recording techniques in animals now allow many thousands of neurons to be recorded simultaneously 27 

(Ahrens et al., 2013; Jun et al., 2017; Saxena & Cunningham, 2019; Steinmetz et al., 2021; Lin et al., 28 

2022; Urai et al., 2022), resulting in complex and high-dimensional data. Decoding analyses of such 29 

data often require sophisticated machine learning methods to translate these measurements into 30 

meaningful insights about neural representations and function. 31 

However such analyses risk data leakage, which occurs when information used to train the model also 32 

appears in the data used to evaluate performance, even in indirect or inadvertent ways. This can often 33 

inflate, and sometimes counterintuitively deflate, the decoding performance of the model and potentially 34 

alter the biological interpretation of the results. Data leakage is increasingly recognized as a critical 35 

and widespread problem in many areas of science where machine learning techniques are applied, 36 

contributing considerably to reproducibility problems (Ambroise & McLachlan, 2002; Simon et al., 2003; 37

sizes and invalidates inference; selection and evaluation must rely on statistically independent data 40

One route to leakage is data-dependent preprocessing, in which information from held-out samples 49 

can shape transformations applied before model fitting. Such preprocessing pipelines can be broadly 50 

categorized into two types: supervised and unsupervised. Supervised preprocessing uses label or task 51 

information to choose features or fit transformations before decoding, for example selecting neurons 52 

based on responsiveness to the decoded variable, selecting features by their correlation with the 53 

behavioral outcome, or applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to label-defined trial averages. 54 

Statistical learning textbooks emphasize that any supervised transformation should be fit exclusively on 55 

training data within each CV fold to avoid contaminating test estimates (Hastie et al., 2009; Abu-Mostafa 56 

et al., 2012). This is a recurrent pitfall that almost universally leads to optimistic performance estimates 57 

if not handled correctly within CV folds. However, a more subtle issue is unsupervised preprocessing, 58 

which uses only the neural activity for transformations such as dimensionality reduction or feature 59 

scaling. While often considered safe (Hastie et al., 2009), recent theoretical work shows that applying 60 

these methods to the entire dataset before partitioning introduces a systematic bias (Moscovich & 61 

Rosset, 2022). Crucially, and counter-intuitively, this bias is not universally optimistic; the estimated error 62 

can also be pessimistic, i.e. leading to an underestimation of a model’s true decoding performance, 63 

depending on a complex interplay between the data, the preprocessing method, and the learning 64 

algorithm. A second route to leakage is temporal dependence, where test samples are not statistically 65 

independent of training samples (Kapoor & Narayanan, 2023). This issue is especially relevant for neural 66 

recordings, where slow internal-state signals and autocorrelated activity can make nearby training and 67 

test samples statistically dependent (Harris & Thiele, 2011; Cowley et al., 2020). Such slow structure 68 

can also produce spurious associations when analyzed with methods that assume independent samples 69 

(Harris, 2021). 70 

Here, we present a systematic analysis of neural decoding pipelines that span supervised and 71 

unsupervised preprocessing, modern dimensionality-reduction methods, and static and temporally 72 

structured data, benchmarking cross-validated estimates against true generalization. Beyond prior 73 

work on feature selection and general-purpose machine-learning pipelines, we focus on preprocessing 74 

operations that are central to systems neuroscience, demonstrating that dimensionality reduction can 75 

bias decoding performance estimates in either direction. We also derive new analytical results showing 76 

how trial-averaged PCA can create separability under random labels, how leaked test samples alter 77 

squared-bias and variance, and how train–test covariance biases test error on temporally correlated 78 

data. 79 

Together, these results demonstrate that data leakage can occur surprisingly easily in neural decoding. 80 

We provide recommendations and open-source code to aid researchers in avoiding data leakage, and 81 

also suggest a simple method for detecting the presence of data leakage. 82

## 2 Results 83 

2.1 The role of neural decoding and the challenge of data leakage 84 

Neural decoding treats the relationship between population activity and stimuli or behavior as a 85 

supervised prediction task (Fig. 1a): a model is trained on neural activity to predict labels (Fig. 1b) 86 

and evaluated on held-out data (Fig. 1c), with performance interpreted as evidence that the population 87 

carries information about the target variable (Borst & Theunissen, 1999; Dayan & Abbott, 2005). 88 

Performance is typically estimated with CV, which partitions data into train/test (and sometimes 89 

validation) sets (Fig. 1d,e) under an assumption of independence between what is used for training and 90 

what is used for evaluation. Data leakage violates this assumption when information from the test set 91 

influences any stage of model training. A frequent source of leakage is preprocessing performed on the 92 

entire dataset before splitting (Fig. 1f). The correct procedure is to split first, then fit all data-dependent 93 

preprocessing and model parameters using only the training data in each fold, and finally apply the 94 

learned transformations to the test data (Fig. 1g). While the potential for data leakage in supervised 95 

preprocessing methods (which use labels) is relatively clear, surprisingly, leakage can also occur from 96 

unsupervised methods. We now discuss both in more detail. Throughout, we use “sample” to mean 97 

one observation supplied to the preprocessor or decoder, and “trial” to mean one experimentally defined 98 

episode, such as a stimulus presentation, choice, or behavioral run. In stimulus-evoked analyses a trial 99 

often contributes a single sample, whereas in continuous behavior or navigation tasks a single trial may 100 

contain many time-resolved samples. 101 

2.2 Data leakage via supervised preprocessing 102 

2.2.1 Supervised feature selection 103 

Feature selection aims to retain neurons informative about the variable of interest while discarding noisy 104 

or uninformative neurons. Common supervised approaches include fitting tuning curves, computing 105 

neuron-label correlations, regression coefficients or ANOVA F-statistics; additional methods include L1 106 

(lasso) regularization, tree-based models, and sequential selection (Ferri et al., 1994; Tibshirani, 1996; 107 

Lotte et al., 2018). Although valuable in the settings of high-dimensional neural data, applying any 108 

label-dependent selection step to the full dataset before CV can induce data leakage (Poldrack et al., 109 

2020; Shim et al., 2021; Rosenblatt et al., 2024), and in at least one case, contributed to a retraction 110 

(Verstynen & Kording, 2023). 111 

Here, “feature selection” refers to the post hoc rule used to decide which recorded neurons enter the 112 

decoder (not e.g. to whether a neuron was physically present on the electrode or in the imaging plane). 113 

Leakage occurs when the statistic used for this decision is estimated from the same samples that will 114 

later be treated as held out. We first illustrate this with a simulation. Neural responses to two stimuli 115 

were generated from multivariate Gaussian distributions with matched covariance and slightly different 116 

means (two-neuron example illustrated in Fig. 2a), each neuron had a Gaussian tuning curve with the 117 

same tuning width but a different tuned stimulus. The theoretical optimal classification accuracy in this 118 

case is 69% (Methods). We increased the number of recorded neurons while keeping the total number 119 

of stimulus presentations fixed, mimicking trial-limited experiments. To remove potentially noisy neurons 120 

and reduce computation time, feature selection was employed to select high-quality neurons (Fig. 2b). 121 

ANOVA F-statistics were computed separately for each neuron, then used to rank the population and 122 

select the top 100 neurons before 5-fold CV. When this ranking was performed on the entire dataset, 123 

the selected set could change when held-out trials were included. Decoding accuracy then increased 124 

with dimensionality but also exceeded the theoretical optimum (Fig. 2c). The same qualitative inflation 125 

occurred without the Gaussian tuning curve assumption (Fig. S1a). This inflation arose because neurons 126 

were chosen using information from all samples, including those later assigned to test folds. Thus, 127

We next examined a real-world example from the visual system. Two-photon calcium imaging of 137

presented at discrete locations to the right side of the visual field (Fig. 2e; see Methods). We asked 139

receives no direct retinal input. Again ANOVA F-statistics were computed on the entire dataset to 142 

select the top 100 neurons, a linear decoder was then trained on their neural activity, and the accuracy 143 

from CV reported. Unsurprisingly decoding performance in the contralateral tectum was very good 144

work has identified a population of intertectal neurons in zebrafish that transmit information between 146 

hemispheres (Gebhardt et al., 2019), providing a possible substrate for the transfer of spatial information 147 

to the ipsilateral tectum. However alas the decoding result is wrong, for the same reasons as in our 148 

simulated example: the test set’s information influenced the selection of features, leading to a biased 149 

training process where the model ‘learned’ from the data it was supposed to predict. When the decoding 150 

analysis was repeated without data leakage – by deriving the features to be selected only on the 151 

training set – decoding accuracy in the ipsilateral tectum fell to chance level (Fig. 2f), indicating that 152 

no spatial information was transferred. Analogous issues for label-free criteria, such as variance- or 153 

firing-rate-based selection, are treated separately below as examples of unsupervised feature selection. 154 155 

2.2.2 Supervised dimensionality reduction 156 

Dimensionality reduction is often used before decoding because neural recordings are commonly 157 

high-dimensional relative to the number of trials. Restricting the decoder to a lower-dimensional 158 

representation can denoise shared population activity, reduce estimator variance, and act as a form of 159 

regularization (Cunningham & Yu, 2014; Mignacco et al., 2020; Green & Romanov, 2025). Empirically, 160 

several neural population studies have used supervised dimensionality reduction before decoding and 161 

reported improved predictive performance compared to using the raw population activity in its original 162 

dimensionality (e.g. Rumyantsev et al., 2020; Ebrahimi et al., 2022). Supervised dimensionality 163 

reduction learns label-informed projections of population activity (Perich et al., 2025). When the 164 

projection is fit on the entire dataset before CV, test-set structure shapes the latent space, making 165 

conditions appear more separable and distorting reported decoding error estimates. 166 

To show that globally-fit supervised dimensionality reduction inflates empirical performance and 167 

that fold-wise fitting removes this underestimation of error, we first simulated neural activity with 168 

independently generated neuronal responses and randomly assigned stimulus labels. Trial-averaged 169 

PCA (a supervised step because averaging uses labels) was used to obtain low-dimensional 170 

embeddings. Here, trial averaging first converts the trial-by-neuron activity into a condition-by-neuron 171 

matrix of class means, where each row is the mean response across all trials of one stimulus condition; 172 

PCA is then computed on this matrix of condition means, yielding axes of maximal variance across 173 

the estimated class means. Individual trials are subsequently projected onto these PC axes for 174

Two possible reasons could explain the optimistic RMSE estimates observed when supervised 193 

projections are fit globally. First, leaked held-out points could improve the fitted model by helping it 194 

estimate a more accurate projection. Second, the model itself might not improve, but the reported 195 

CV error could become optimistic because the evaluation points had already influenced the projection 196 

used to predict them. To distinguish between these possibilities, we used a simulated linear regression 197 

problem with known ground-truth coefficients β and one-component Partial Least Squares (PLS) as the 198 

supervised dimensionality-reduction step. For centered data, this PLS step learned a one-dimensional 199 

projection from the covariance between features and labels; a linear regressor was then fit on the 200 

projected training data. For simplicity, in the analysis below we considered the case in which one point 201 

was held out at a time in each CV loop. Across training sample sizes, we compared the following three 202 

diagnostic quantities: (i) leaky CV, where the PLS projection was fit using the training data plus the 203 

held-out test point, but the linear regressor was fit only on the projected training data; (ii) non-leaky 204 

CV, where both PLS and the regressor were fit using training data only and then evaluated on held-out 205 

points; and (iii) the true generalization error of the model from (i), evaluated on independent new samples 206 

that were not used to fit either the PLS projection or the regressor. Because the true β was known 207 

in this simulation, we also measured parameter RMSE: the error between the estimated regression 208 

coefficients, mapped back to the original feature space, and β. This ground-truth comparison directly 209 

measured coefficient recovery, whereas CV RMSE measured prediction error on samples designated 210 

as held out by the CV split. 211 

These results support the second explanation above, rather than genuine improvement in the estimator. 212 

The leaky and non-leaky pipelines produced nearly identical parameter RMSE (Fig. 3g), indicating that 213 

the leaked point did not materially improve recovery of the known generative coefficients. However, 214 

the leaky CV RMSE underestimated the true generalization error of that same leaky model, whereas 215 

non-leaky CV aligned with the generalization-error curve (Fig. 3h). Thus, the apparent improvement 216 

in leaky CV error arose from violated independence between preprocessing and evaluation, not from 217

inflates prediction error. The leaky CV RMSE sits below the asymptote because the held-out point’s 222

2.3 Data leakage via unsupervised preprocessing 228 

A critical, yet often overlooked, form of data leakage can arise during unsupervised preprocessing 229 

from the features (neural activity) themselves. Standard methods in data analysis pipelines, such 230 

as dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA), feature scaling, or variance-based feature selection, can 231 

introduce error-estimation bias if they are applied to the entire dataset before partitioning for CV 232 

(here and elsewhere unless otherwise noted, error-estimation bias refers to the mismatch between 233 

a cross-validated error estimate and the true generalization error of the same model, distinct from 234 

the squared-bias term in the bias–variance decomposition below.) The application of unsupervised 235 

methods across the full dataset is a more insidious pitfall, partly because of contradictory suggestions 236 

in leading statistics textbooks (Hastie et al., 2009, p. 246 vs. Abu-Mostafa et al., 2012, p. 174). 237 

Recent theoretical work provides a rigorous foundation for understanding this problem. In particular, 238 

Moscovich & Rosset (2022) demonstrated analytically that applying data-dependent unsupervised 239 

transformations globally can make the cross-validated error estimate differ systematically from the 240 

model’s true generalization error. Crucially, they showed that this mismatch is not universally optimistic. 241 

Its sign and magnitude depend on an intricate interplay between the data distribution, the specific 242 

preprocessing method, the subsequent modeling algorithm, and the CV parameters. We next examined 243 

this bidirectional (optimistic or pessimistic) deviation from true generalization error across common 244 

unsupervised steps in neural decoding: feature selection, z-scoring, PCA, and modern unsupervised 245 

dimensionality reduction algorithms. A bias–variance decomposition of Principal Component Regression 246 

(PCR) serves as a case study to explain when and why the sign of the leakage-induced mismatch 247 

reverses. 248 

2.3.1 Unsupervised feature selection 249 

To investigate the practical impact of this phenomenon in a common neural decoding context, we 250 

simulated neural responses to a circular stimulus variable (e.g., orientation or direction of motion) 251 

using the Gamma-Poisson generative model of Pillow & Scott (2012) (Fig. 4a). In the main simulation, 252 

each neuron had a Gaussian tuning curve over stimulus angle, so stimulus-dependent modulation was 253 

defined as part of the response-generation model. We first confirmed that without any feature selection, 254 

a simple linear decoder could be trained to effectively predict the stimulus angle from the population 255 

activity, establishing that the decoding problem is well-posed (Fig. 4b). We then implemented a natural 256 

preprocessing workflow for high-dimensional neural recordings: we performed unsupervised feature 257 

selection by selecting the subset of neurons with the highest spike count variance across the entire 258 

dataset before CV. This criterion is intuitive, as neurons with higher variance are generally those whose 259 

firing rates are more strongly modulated by the different stimuli, making them more informative for 260 

decoding. However, when this ranking is computed once on the full dataset, held-out trials have already 261 

influenced which neurons enter the decoder. The procedure is therefore leaky even though it does not 262 

use stimulus labels. 263 

The leaky model’s estimated error from the CV procedure consistently deviated from its true 264 

generalization error (Fig. 4c). Intuitively, this mismatch reflected a bias–variance trade-off: scoring 265 

neurons on the full dataset partially fit the held-out trials’ fluctuations, which lowered apparent 266 

squared-bias but inflated prediction variance, with the net sign set by whichever effect dominated. The 267

We next asked when this unsupervised-selection leakage becomes large enough to matter in practice. 278 

We compared two variance-based ranking rules by performing a Gamma-Poisson neuron-count sweep 279 

with 100 samples, 5-fold CV, Gaussian tuning curves, and selection of the top 10% of neurons. “Evoked” 280 

selection ranked neurons using the stimulus-evoked samples that entered the decoding analysis, 281 

whereas “spontaneous” selection ranked neurons using stimulus-independent activity outside the 282 

evoked decoding samples. With evoked selection, the leaky-CV/generalization error ratio remained 283 

close to one for small populations but increased as the total recorded population approached hundreds 284 

of neurons (Fig. 4d). In contrast, spontaneous selection kept the error ratio close to one (Fig. 4d). The 285 

same qualitative contrast between evoked and spontaneous selection was observed when neurons were 286 

ranked by mean firing rate rather than variance (Fig. S6d). The sampling noise in each neuron’s mean or 287 

variance estimate is mainly determined by the number of trials, not by the number of recorded neurons. 288 

The population-size dependence arises at the ranking step: with more candidate neurons, random 289 

fluctuations from the held-out folds have more chances to change which neurons are selected. Thus, 290 

the leaky and training-only selected feature sets are expected to be similar in low-dimensional, trial-rich 291 

settings, but can diverge in trial-limited, high-dimensional recordings. A simplified Gaussian-feature 292 

calculation with an ordinary least squares decoder gives the same qualitative prediction: the ratio is 293 

bounded, but increases as the number of selected neurons approaches the number of training samples 294 

per fold. For the 100 samples, selecting the top 10% neurons from 750 neurons gives an expected 295 

error ratio of approximately 1.12 in this Gaussian benchmark (Section S3). Thus, criteria specified 296 

independently of the evoked decoding samples need not be nested within CV, whereas scores computed 297 

from those samples should be recomputed within each training fold. 298 

2.3.2 Unsupervised dimensionality reduction 299 

Another common and powerful unsupervised preprocessing step in neural decoding is dimensionality 300 

reduction. Unsupervised dimensionality reduction algorithms identify patterns and structures in data 301 

without the need for labeled outcomes or predictions (Kriegeskorte & Wei, 2021; Langdon et al., 2023). 302 

They can uncover hidden structures and correlations within complex, high-dimensional datasets and are 303 

particularly important when dealing with spontaneous neural activity or unlabelled naturalistic behavior, 304 

facilitating the discovery of intrinsic neural representations and functional connectivity without prior 305 

knowledge. 306 

PCA is a cornerstone of many neural data analysis pipelines, providing a linear mapping from 307 

high-dimensional activity to lower-dimensional components (Fig. 5a). We first investigated how data 308 

leakage affects PCR, in which PCA precedes linear regression. In a leaky CV pipeline, PCA was fit 309 

using the training samples plus the held-out sample in each CV loop, whereas the regression model 310 

was fit only on the projected training samples. In the non-leaky CV pipeline, both PCA and regression 311 

were fit using only the training samples. The resulting regression parameters had comparable quality 312 

between the two pipelines (Fig. 5b), and the sample mean squared error (MSE) from the non-leaky 313 

CV closely matched the true generalization error of the leaky pipeline (Fig. 5c). In contrast, the 314

trials are included during dimensionality reduction. Neural spikes were simulated from a two-dimensional 339 

latent angular variable, and GPFA recovered 2D latent trajectories (Fig. 5k) from the binned firing rate 340 

across many simulated trials. Such trajectories were then used with a k-nearest-neighbor regressor 341 

(k=5) to decode angle (Fig. 5l). Fitting GPFA globally before CV leaked test information, producing 342 

over-optimistic decoding error relative to an approximate generalization error (Fig. 5m). In contrast, 343 

fold-wise refitting removed leakage and yielded CV errors that matched generalization error (Fig. 5m). 344 

To demonstrate these effects on real neural data, we re-analyzed two population recordings from visual 345 

cortex of awake mice (Stringer et al., 2021). In the first dataset, the original pipeline z-scored and 346 

applied PCA on the full dataset. Refitting both steps within each CV fold produced a small but consistent 347 

increase in decoding error, revealing that a leaky pipeline where z-scoring and PCA were applied 348 

globally optimistically underestimated error (Fig. 5n), with sign consistent with our PCR bias–variance 349 

analysis in the high SNR regime (Fig. 5e,f). In the second dataset, for which the original pipeline 350 

(Fig. 2H of Stringer et al. (2021)) used z-scoring as the only preprocessing step, correcting this single 351 

source of leakage consistently reduced median decoding error across animals (Fig. 5o). Because 352 

the original recordings contained thousands of stimuli, these corrections changed the error estimates 353 

only modestly and did not alter the qualitative conclusions of the original study. However, when we 354 

downsampled the same recordings to tens or hundreds of stimuli, a regime closer to many other neural 355 

decoding experiments, the difference between leaky and fold-wise preprocessing became substantially 356 

larger (Fig. S7). Similar to previous analysis on decoding the animal location (Fig. 3f), we then used 357 

unsupervised CEBRA (Schneider et al., 2023) for dimensionality reduction, and again we observed 358 

that the leaky pipeline optimistically underestimated error (Fig. 5p). This confirmed that data leakage 359 

via unsupervised preprocessing can unpredictably skew model evaluation in real-world neuroscience 360 

applications, reinforcing the need for correctly implemented, within-fold preprocessing to ensure valid 361 

and reproducible results. 362

2.4 Data leakage from temporal correlations in neural time-series 363 

Beyond preprocessing, data leakage can also arise from how samples are split for CV. CV assumes 364 

that held-out samples are independent of the samples used for training, however neural time series often 365 

violate this condition. Autocorrelation can arise, for instance, from the slow biophysical kinetics of calcium 366 

indicators, population-level synchronous dynamics reflected in LFP and EEG signals, refractory periods, 367 

or slowly varying internal states (Harris & Thiele, 2011; Cowley et al., 2020). Such temporal structure 368 

can affect decoding analyses in two distinct ways. First, shared slow temporal structure between task 369 

variables and neural activity can produce apparent decoding in the absence of genuine task encoding. 370 

The pseudosession framework of Harris (2021) addresses this problem by constructing empirical null 371 

distributions that test whether neural activity contains task information not explained by design-related 372 

temporal structure. Second, when neural activity carries task information, randomized splitting can 373 

underestimate decoding error by placing temporally adjacent samples in both training and test sets. 374 

To address this second failure mode, we simulated calcium-imaging data with within-trial temporal 375 

correlations but no across-trial correlations. Spiking activity was generated from neuronal tuning curves 376 

encoding an angular variable, then convolved with a calcium-indicator kernel, producing autocorrelated 377 

fluorescence traces (Fig. 6a). Randomized k-fold CV (randomized CV) split individual time points 378 

across training and test sets, reflecting the statistical coupling that arises whenever random splitting 379 

places temporally dependent samples from the same trial into different folds (Fig. 6b). By contrast, 380 

leave-one-group-out CV (LOGO CV) held out entire independent trials, ensuring that temporally adjacent 381 

samples from a single trial were not split across folds (Fig. 6c). In both schemes, preprocessing and 382 

hyperparameter selection were restricted to the training data. Therefore, any mismatch between 383 

cross-validated and generalization error reflected temporal dependence induced by the split rather 384 

than data leakage from model selection or preprocessing. LOGO CV estimates more closely matched 385 

generalization error than randomized CV, indicating that randomized CV produced optimistic error 386 

estimates (Fig. 6d). 387 

The covariance structure makes the source of the bias explicit. Under randomized CV, within-trial 388 

autocorrelation produced non-zero train ×test entries in the covariance matrix, showing that held-out 389 

samples were statistically coupled to training samples (Fig. 6e). Under LOGO CV, held-out samples 390 

came from an independent trial. Because cross-trial correlations were zero in the simulation, the 391 

train ×test block vanished (Fig. 6f). Thus, leakage through temporal correlation can persist even when 392 

all fitting operations are confined to training data, because the split itself determines whether the test set 393 

is independent. 394 

In a simplified binary-discrimination task, we then tested whether train–test covariance was sufficient to 395 

explain the error-estimation difference. The class labels of the stimuli contained no slow structure and 396 

were independent of time, so the difference only arose because autocorrelated, task-irrelevant noise 397 

made held-out samples statistically predictable from nearby training samples. An analytical calculation 398 

for a linear decoder showed that the relevant quantity is the average covariance between each held-out 399 

sample and the training samples used to estimate the decision boundary (see Methods). Specifically, 400 

while the generalization error remained determined by the true class separation, strong train–test 401 

covariance could make apparent leaky test error lower than the true generalization error. Consistent 402 

with this, when class separation was adjusted to hold the apparent leaky test error fixed, increasing 403 

train–test temporal correlation produced different true generalization errors (Fig. 6g). 404 

We then asked whether this covariance structure could be used as a practical diagnostic. We varied the 405 

size of contiguous train/test blocks, which changes the train–test covariance while leaving the marginal 406 

noise model and decoder fixed. Smaller blocks placed more training and test samples close together 407 

in time, whereas larger blocks separated them more strongly. For each split, we estimated temporal 408 

covariance from residual activity and used the train–test covariance block to correct the leaky test 409

## 3 Discussion 414 

Here we systematically examined how data leakage can arise in common neural decoding pipelines 415 

and how it biases empirical performance estimates. The empirical analyses are complemented by 416 

derivations that explain why these biases arise. Using a range of supervised and unsupervised 417 

preprocessing methods, we showed that fitting preprocessing steps on the entire dataset before CV 418 

can lead to optimistic or pessimistic errors that do not reflect true generalization performance. Across 419 

simulations and real datasets, we demonstrated that properly implemented train-test splits, in which all 420 

parameters of the preprocessing and decoding pipeline are learned only on the training data within each 421 

fold, restore calibration between cross-validated error and generalization error. In the Recommendations 422 

section (Section S1) we outline simple templates for leak-free neural decoding pipelines, and we 423 

propose a practical diagnostic to help detect potential leakage in existing analyses. 424 

For temporally correlated recordings, preventing leakage also requires attention to the unit over which 425 

independence is approximated. Preprocessing only within each fold is necessary, but it does not ensure 426 

that held-out samples are independent if nearby time points are split across training and test sets. 427 

Whenever possible, validation should hold out experimental units more likely to be independent, such as 428 

complete trials or sufficiently separated blocks. These units should be chosen to match the scientific 429 

quantity to be estimated. For within-experiment decoding, the goal is usually to evaluate held-out 430 

samples from the same experimental context while minimizing train-test dependence. When no natural 431 

independent unit is available, block-size sensitivity analyses and covariance-based diagnostics can help 432 

assess whether the reported decoding error depends on residual train-test covariance. However, these 433 

analyses should be treated as diagnostics rather than substitutes for a well-designed validation split. 434 

Our focus has been on preventing data leakage when training and evaluation data are intended to 435 

represent the same target distribution. This is distinct from out-of-distribution generalization, which asks 436 

whether a decoder transfers across recording days, subjects, tasks, or other experimental contexts. A 437 

decoder may be well calibrated for appropriately held-out trials within one experiment yet fail in a new 438 

context, and such failure reflects limited generalization rather than data leakage. Researchers should be 439 

explicit about whether their conclusions concern information content within a fixed experimental context, 440 

or claims about robustness across contexts. 441 

Current intracortical BCI research actively targets this generalization challenge. To maintain a fixed 442 

decoder despite recording instabilities, methods align latent dynamics or full-dimensional neural 443 

distributions across recording days (Degenhart et al., 2020; Karpowicz et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2023). 444 

However these alignment techniques are themselves data-dependent, even when unsupervised. Thus 445 

hyperparameter selection, alignment method choice, and any representation update must be validated 446 

without access to the held-out evaluation day. 447 

The emergence of large-scale neural foundation models trained on extensive datasets from multiple 448 

subjects and experimental conditions (e.g. Ye et al. (2023); Azabou et al. (2023, 2025); Wang et al. 449 

(2025)) presents both opportunities and challenges for preventing data leakage. These models provide 450 

pre-trained representations that capture generalizable neural patterns across diverse experimental 451 

contexts and subjects. To apply these models to new data, researchers typically need to adapt the 452 

pre-trained representations through self-supervised (or supervised) learning. These adaptation steps 453 

can include hyperparameter selection, normalization, alignment, and test-time adaptation. Each is 454

effectively another form of preprocessing, where the model learns to map new neural data into the 455 

pre-trained representation space. Pretraining on external data does not itself introduce leakage, 456 

provided the held-out test set is excluded from the representation update. However, if the foundation 457 

model is adapted using the entire dataset before splitting into training and test sets, information from the 458 

test set will contaminate the representation learning process. For claims about held-out samples within 459 

one recording, adaptation steps must be fit without those held-out samples. For transfer claims across 460 

recording days, animals, subjects, or tasks, the evaluation unit being tested should remain outside the 461 

adaptation process. The only exception is when test-time adaptation is itself the deployment setting; in 462 

that case, the adaptation rule should be specified in advance and assessed on a further, completely 463 

unseen dataset. 464 

The same principles extend beyond neural decoding. Recent work on alignment between large language 465 

models and the brain showed that shuffled train-test splits and overlooked confounds can create apparent 466 

brain-model correspondence that is not robust under stricter evaluation (Hadidi et al., 2026). As these 467 

pipelines grow in complexity, so do the opportunities for data leakage, making it essential to verify that 468 

every data-dependent step is isolated from the held-out data for evaluation. 469

The accuracy of this prediction is used to quantify decoder performance. (d) Standard CV data partitioning. A dataset is split into training (green) and test (blue) sets over multiple folds. (e) An optional validation set (mauve) can be used for hyperparameter tuning. (f) An incorrect, “leaky” data pipeline. Preprocessing is applied to the whole dataset (grey hatching across the full bar) and the resulting processed data are then split into training and test sets (green and blue segments with the same hatching), so the test data influence the learned preprocessing and contaminate the training set, leading to biased performance estimates. (g) A corrected non-leaky data pipeline. The dataset is first partitioned into training and test folds. Preprocessing parameters are then learned only from the training data (green segment with hatching) and the same transformation is applied to the held-out test data (blue segment with matching hatching), ensuring that test data do not affect how preprocessing is learned.

Figure 6: Data leakage from inappropriate CV of autocorrelated neural time series . (a) : Example of a continuous, autocorrelated neural signal, such as that found in EEG, LFP, or calcium imaging data (simulated calcium trace shown here). The signal’s value at any given time point is highly correlated with its value at adjacent time points. (b) : Two flawed validation schemes using standard randomized k-fold CV (randomized CV). Data points were assigned to training (blue) and test (orange) sets randomly, which violated their temporal structure and trial-based independence. (c) : Given the trial-based independence in the simulation, a more methodologically sound approach using leave-one-group(trial)-out CV (LOGO CV). Entire independent trials were held out as the test set. This correctly preserved the temporal structure within each trial while ensuring no temporal dependence existed between the training and test data. For both methods, hyperparameter optimization was performed using a nested CV loop (omitted from illustration for clarity). (d) : Randomized CV led to artificially inflated performance metrics. The decoder evaluated with the randomized CV (“leaky”) method showed a significantly lower RMSE than one evaluated with LOGO CV (“non-leaky”) However, the true generalization error, assessed on a separate, held-out test set, revealed that the leaky method’s superior performance was an artifact. LOGO CV, in contrast, provided an accurate estimate of how the model would perform on new, unseen data. (e) :Covariance matrix of the simulated signal from Fold 1 under randomized CV setting in (b), reordered so that training samples precede test samples on both axes. Within-trial autocorrelation produced non-zero entries in the off-diagonal train ×test block, reflecting statistical dependence between the training and test sets that inflated the apparent decoding performance. The second scheme in (b) has higher averaged temporal correlation than the first scheme. (f) : The same covariance reordered from any single fold under LOGO CV in (c). Because the held-out trial shared no samples with any training trial and cross-trial correlations were zero, the train ×test block vanished, indicating statistically independent training and test sets. (g) : In a simplified binary-decoding setting, the same apparent leaky test error corresponded to different true generalization errors as the train–test temporal correlation varied. Lower values along the horizontal axis indicate weaker train–test correlation, whereas higher values indicate stronger correlation.

(h) : Covariance-based corrections brought the leaky estimate closer to performance on independent data across train/test block sizes; the estimated corrections were closer to the true generalization error than the naive leaky test estimate. The scalar correction ceff collapsed the per-sample train–test covariance reductions to their class-averaged mean and applied a closed-form variance adjustment; the full correction instead retained the per-sample leakage vector and numerically inverted the test-error equation to recover the generalization variance (see Methods and Section S5). S1.2 Detection methods 972 

We suggest a simple practical check to test for data leakage. An effective approach for determining the 973 

chance level in neural decoding analysis is through permutation tests, also known as shuffle controls 974 

(Ojala & Garriga, 2010; Combrisson & Jerbi, 2015). This method involves permuting stimulus identities 975 

or neural activity across classes to establish an empirical null distribution of decoding accuracy, providing 976 

a more accurate estimate than the naive 1/(number of unique stimuli) calculation. For a balanced 977 

classification problem, if a shuffle control is performed on an entire decoding pipeline which involves data 978 

leakage, the observed null distribution will be different than the 1/(number of unique stimuli) estimate. 979 

However in typical neuroscience analyses, a shuffle control is performed just before decoding. The null 980 

distribution constructed this way does not incorporate preprocessing steps, making it less informative 981 

than a null distribution constructed by performing the shuffle control at the start of the pipeline. We 982 

therefore recommend conducting permutation tests prior to data preprocessing rather than afterwards. 983 

Since preprocessing parameters are derived from the data itself, this step should be thought of as part 984 

of the machine learning classifier. This approach is analogous to introducing randomness at the start 985 

of simulations as a null model (for example, in network science, random graphs are constructed as a 986 

null model (Barabási & Albert, 1999; Fornito et al., 2016; Váša & Miši´ c, 2022)). This practice also aligns 987 

with best practices in popular machine learning projects, as exemplified by the API design for scikit-learn 988 

(Buitinck et al., 2013). However note that while this check is very useful in practice, it should be viewed 989 

as a screening tool rather than a definitive guarantee: passing it reduces concern about leakage, but 990 

failing it is a clear warning sign that the pipeline needs to be audited. 991 

S1.3 Hyperparameter tuning 992 

When hyperparameter tuning is required, nested cross-validation provides a more robust approach than 993 

standard cross-validation (Stone, 1974; Cawley & Talbot, 2010). In nested cross-validation, an outer loop 994 

performs the train-test split for final evaluation, while an inner loop performs additional train-validation 995 

splits for hyperparameter optimization. This ensures that the hyperparameter selection process does not 996 

inadvertently use information from the test set, which could occur if hyperparameters are tuned on the 997 

entire dataset before cross-validation. The computational cost of nested cross-validation is higher, but it 998 

provides more reliable estimates of model performance, particularly when preprocessing steps involve 999 

hyperparameters (such as the number of components in PCA or regularization strength in many machine 1000 

learning models). 1001

S4.9 Varying number of features via a master model 1220 

So far in this section, the primary simulation setting assumes a fixed feature dimension p and focuses on 1221

S5.8 Summary 1386 

Temporal autocorrelations intrinsically violate the independence assumptions of standard cross-validation, 1387 

producing a predictable, downward-biased estimate of generalization error. As we have shown, this 1388 

bias is driven by the cross-covariance between the training and test sets. By modeling this temporal 1389 

covariance structure, and applying either the simple scalar correction or the more precise full per-sample 1390 

correction, we can analytically reverse the artificial variance reduction. These correction procedures 1391 

thus offer a practical tool for reporting unbiased decoding performance in continuous neural recordings 1392 

without needing to discard large segments of data to enforce strict independence. 1393
