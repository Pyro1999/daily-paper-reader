The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 23, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.18.733142 doi: bioRxiv preprint 2Abstract 23 Selective attention allows listeners to follow a target speaker in complex scenes, but less is 24 known about the neural mechanisms that control this capacity. We collected 25 electroencephalography (EEG) during an auditory attention task encompassing spatial attention, 26 talker-based attention, and passive listening. Time-resolved representational similarity analysis 27 (RSA) of broadband scalp voltage and alpha-band oscillations revealed distinct representational 28 trajectories in the two measurements: scalp voltage produced brief peaks in task discriminability 29 shortly after the target cue and around 300 ms after target onset, whereas alpha-band power 30 emerged more slowly and remained elevated throughout the trial. These patterns distinguish 31 attentive from passive conditions and spatial from talker attention. Furthermore, broadband 32 activity and alpha power encode complementary information, while other frequency bands 33 contribute little independent explanatory power. These results demonstrate that RSA allows a 34 unified interpretation of evoked and induced EEG measures, revealing how distinct neural 35 computations jointly encode the spatiotemporal dynamics of selective attention. 36 1. Introduction 37 Whether in a busy restaurant trying to order food or attending a Zoom meeting in a cafe, we 38 face the daily challenge of selecting and focusing on one auditory target among many 39 competing sounds. This challenge, known as the “cocktail party problem” (Cherry, 1953), 40 highlights the importance of auditory selective attention—the ability to focus on a specific sound 41 source while ignoring distractions (Alain & Bernstein, 2008). Understanding how auditory 42 selective attention is implemented and controlled is critical to explaining how we perceive 43 speech in noisy environments. Further, because even modest hearing impairment interferes 44 with auditory selective attention (Dai et al., 2018), identifying the mechanisms that support 45 selective listening in noisy environments has substantial clinical relevance. 46 47 Listeners rely on a range of cues to identify and selectively attend to task-relevant speech 48 amidst distractors. One prominent and well-studied cue is talker location. The representation of 49 the location of a sound source first emerges in the brainstem and propagates to auditory cortex; 50 when behaviorally relevant, spatial position can even be decoded in frontal cortex (Blauert, 51 1997; Lewald & Getzmann, 2011; van der Heijden et al., 2019; Middlebrooks, 2021). Another 52 key cue is the voice identity of the speaker, shaped by fundamental acoustic features such as 53 pitch and timbre (Darwin et al., 2003; Oh et al., 2021; Shinn-Cunningham, 2008). Differences in 54 fundamental frequency and vocal tract length allow listeners to distinguish individual talkers 55 even in challenging listening conditions (Darwin et al., 2003). In addition, the structure of the 56 speech signal itself contributes to attentional selection. Temporal features in the speech 57 envelope help listeners track a continuous speech stream (Henry & Obleser, 2012; Obleser & 58 Kayser 2019). While listeners are able to use these different types of cues to focus on task-59 relevant speech, the underlying neural mechanisms through which attention employs each cue 60 remain only partially understood. 61 62

3Electroencephalography (EEG) provides a powerful tool for studying neural activity during 63 auditory attention, primarily due to its high temporal resolution (Näätänen & Picton, 1987; Luck, 64 2014). Attention modulates neural activity through multiple mechanisms, and EEG recordings 65 reflect these modulations (Tavano et al., 2023). For example, auditory event-related potentials 66 (ERPs) are modulated by attention when listeners track one of two competing streams (Choi et 67 al., 2013; Hillyard et al., 1973; Nguyen et al., 2024). Frequency-based features also reflect 68 attentional modulation. Alpha-band power (8-12 Hz) in parietal regions is a hallmark of spatial 69 attention (Kerlin et al., 2010; Wöstmann et al., 2021). Parietal alpha lateralization correlates with 70 spatial cue direction—alpha power increases ipsilateral to the attended location and decreases 71 contralaterally (Deng et al., 2020). Parietal alpha power also has been implicated in non-spatial 72 auditory attention (Wöstmann et al., 2017; Viswanathan et al., 2023). Neural activity in lower 73 frequency bands like delta (2-4 Hz) and theta (4-8 Hz) may also be important in selective 74 processing of attended speech, as they closely track speech features such as temporal 75 envelope (Etard & Reichenbach, 2019; Ding et al., 2016). 76 77 Despite these decades of studies, it remains unclear how various EEG features index the neural 78 mechanisms supporting different aspects of auditory attention. There are a number of 79 challenges. First, the high dimensionality of EEG data makes it challenging to compare 80 condition-related differences in a single view. Different types of auditory attention lead to 81 different patterns of coordinated activity across multiple, spatially distributed brain regions. 82 Decoding these differences requires fine-grained temporal resolution and access to the spatial 83 distribution of neural activity across various EEG features, such as time-domain signals and 84 frequency band power. Second, comparing these EEG features is challenging because they 85 differ in both spatial and temporal characteristics. For instance, ERPs reflect short-latency, 86 stimulus-evoked activity in frontocentral regions, while alpha-band oscillations reflect sustained 87 spatial attention in posterior areas. Third, the magnitude of these features differ, complicating 88 efforts to combine information across them. 89 90 Representational similarity analysis (RSA; Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) offers a unified framework 91 for describing neural activity across conditions and measurement techniques. The approach first 92 computes pairwise similarity between conditions and then infers their representational structure 93 from the resulting pattern of all comparisons. From its initial applications in examining visual 94 representations in the brain using fMRI (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008; Cichy et al., 2014), RSA has 95 since been applied to fMRI data to explore diverse topics such as semantic knowledge and 96 episodic memory (Clarke & Tyler, 2014; Xue, 2018). More recently, RSA has been used to 97 exploit fine-scale temporal information of EEG signals to gain insights into object recognition, 98 audiovisual processing, and reading comprehension (Kaneshiro et al., 2015; Cecere et al., 99 2017; Wang et al., 2020). For example, Cecere et al. (2017) computed time-resolved neural 100 representational patterns to assess the similarity between auditory-leading and visual-leading 101 multimodal stimuli, showing that they elicit distinct neural representational structures. 102 103 A key advantage of RSA emerges when analyzing condition-rich experimental designs; RSA 104 makes it possible to represent many experimental conditions within a single visualization and to 105 compare them within the same analysis framework. Unique contribution analysis 345 Alpha power has been associated with selective attention in many studies (e.g., Deng et al., 346 2020; Kerlin et al., 2010; Wöstmann et al., 2021) and represented attention most strongly in our 347 data (see Results). To assess whether the representation in other EEG features was 348 complementary or redundant to that in alpha power, we compared RDMs derived from dual-349 feature decoding (using both alpha power and one other feature) to those derived using each 350 feature independently. Alpha power was concatenated with one additional feature (EEG scalp 351 voltage, delta-, theta-, beta-, or gamma-band power) along the channel axis, resulting in a 128-352 channel weight vector. A stronger regularization parameter of 0.01 was used to account for the 353 doubled number of inputs. The rest of the parameters and the training procedure remained the 354 same. 355 356 To evaluate how evoked and induced EEG activity each contribute to the representation of 357 attention, we quantified how much decoding accuracy with dual-feature decoding exceeded 358 accuracy with a single feature (Di Liberto et al., 2018). For example, the unique contribution of 359 alpha-band power (induced) relative to broadband voltage (evoked) was computed by 360 subtracting the RDM based on broadband voltage alone from the RDM derived from both 361 features together. Representational structure present in the dual-feature but not the single-362 feature RDM can thus be attributed to the unique contribution of alpha-band oscillations. The 363 converse was done to estimate the unique contribution of evoked activity relative to induced 364 alpha-band activity. As above, we computed dissimilarity indices to summarize these RDMs 365 over time and identified windows of statistically significant contributions using cluster-based 366 permutation testing. 367 368 The main manuscript presents the unique contribution analysis of alpha-band power and 369 broadband scalp voltage; analysis of alpha compared to other frequency bands is presented in 370 the supplemental materials. 371

Thus, we included all trials—both 375 correct and incorrect—in subsequent analyses. Detailed information can be found in An et al. 376 (2023). 377 3.2. Representational Dissimilarity of Attention 378 To probe the spatiotemporal dynamics of attention during a challenging auditory attention task, 379 we measured two neural features (evoked broadband scalp voltage and induced alpha-band 380 power) and computed timepoint by timepoint representational dissimilarity in two epochs: the 381 Cue Period epoch spanning the task cue, target cue, and the time leading up to stimulus 382 presentation, and Stimulus Period epoch surrounding the presentation of the target syllable (see 383 Methods). 384 385 3.2.1. Cue Period 386 We first examined the Cue Period during which listeners prepared to deploy selective attention. 387 Neural representations reflected in evoked broadband scalp voltage and in induced alpha-band 388 power showed distinct time dynamics. Scalp voltage (Figure 4a–c) exhibited a rapid increase 389 immediately after the target cue, followed by a decline over time, whereas alpha-band power 390 showed a more gradual increase that persisted through the Cue Period. 391 392 In broadband scalp voltage, there was no representation of task before the onset of any trial-393 specific information, 1100 ms before target cue onset. The attentive-passive dissimilarity index 394 became significantly positive approximately 400 ms after task cue onset and remained elevated 395 for nearly the entire epoch. The space-talker dissimilarity index hovered near zero until the 396 target cue occurred. Both dissimilarity indices peaked around 100–200 ms after target cue onset 397 and then decreased over time. Unique contribution analysis revealed that these two types of neural activity carried 553 complementary representations, each encoding information beyond that carried by the other. 554 555 By applying RSA to internal cognitive states, this study examines the neural representations of 556 task goals and attentional control. We minimized effects of acoustic difference across conditions 557 by carefully matching the auditory stimuli while varying the cue dimension that would identify the 558 target. This design allowed us to isolate changes in representation that were attributable to 559 internal task goals and differences in how attention was deployed. 560 Neural representations in broadband scalp voltage show patterns similar to 561 auditory event-related potentials. 562 Transient representational differences between attention and passive listening conditions were 563 apparent in broadband voltage shortly after the auditory cue (Figures 4a–c). Previous work has 564 shown that attention modulates auditory evoked responses within 100-300 ms of sound onset 565 (Choi et al., 2013; Hillyard et al., 1973; Nguyen et al., 2024, Näätänen & Picton, 1987), which is 566 the time window in which we observed this effect. In the sensory gain control framework 567 (Hillyard et al., 1973, Hillyard et al., 1998), attention selectively modulates sensory responses in 568 auditory cortex such that evoked responses to a particular sound are relatively larger when that 569 sound is attended compared to when it is ignored. Our spatial importance maps showed 570 relatively higher classifier weights in frontocentral channels at 150 ms and 350 ms post-cue 571 when comparing attentive to passive listening. These channels typically show attentional 572 modulation of auditory evoked responses in traditional EEG experiments (Hillyard et al., 1973; 573 Näätänen & Picton, 1987). 574 575 Throughout stimulus presentation, the attentive-passive dissimilarity was reliably significant, but 576 dissimilarity increased from approximately 300 ms after target onset (Figure 6a). This time 577 window is consistent with the evoked P300 target-detection response, which arises in centro-578 parietal channels after a task  relevant target is detected (Donchin & Coles, 1988). The P300 579 may reflect working memory updates when important or task-relevant information is perceived 580 (Polich, 2007; Watter et al., 2001; Guerrero et al., 2023). Consistent with this interpretation, 581 spatial importance maps show high central-parietal weights in attentive-passive contrasts at this 582 time. However, other processes such as motor planning or short-term memory maintenance 583 may also contribute to this response (Verleger et al., 2005; Ruchkin et al., 1992). 584 585 Representational differences between spatial and talker attention were only reliably significant 586 shortly after the target identity cue (around 100–300 ms, see Figure 4a). However, the target 587 identity cues were not as carefully matched as other stimulus parameters. In spatial attention 588 trials, this cue was lateralized to the target location with a neutral voice; in talker attention and 589 passive listening trials, this cue was presented at the center with the same voice as the target 590 syllable. Representations in alpha power were 602 more distinctive than those in any other EEG feature, consistent with many prior findings linking 603 alpha oscillations to attention (e.g., Deng et al., 2020; Kerlin et al., 2010; Wöstmann et al., 2021; 604 Klimesch, 2012). 605 606 Alpha-band oscillations have been linked to a gating mechanism that shields ongoing cognitive 607 processing from interference (Jensen & Mazaheri, 2010; Foxe & Snyder, 2011; Strauß et al., 608 2014). Alpha power measured at the scalp likely reflects several different neural generators that 609 are engaged during selective attention (Deng et al., 2020; Wöstmann et al., 2017; Gutteling et 610 al., 2022; see review in Jensen & Bonnefond, 2026). Posterior parietal and occipital alpha is 611 strongly related to spatial attention to a specific hemifield, with alpha power increasing 612 ipsilaterally and decreasing contralaterally to the target location, as if it is suppressing inputs at 613 task-irrelevant locations (Deng et al., 2020; Wöstmann et al., 2019). Beyond spatial orienting, 614 alpha power also reflects more general aspects of attentional demand and cognitive control 615 (Wöstmann et al., 2017, 2019; Gutteling et al., 2022). For example, increases in posterior alpha 616 accompany higher acoustic detail or disturbance in the distractor stream, consistent with a role 617 in shielding target processing from interfering input (Wöstmann et al., 2019). Further, parieto-618 occipital alpha power predicts trial-wise speech intelligibility in competing sounds, linking this 619 shielding mechanism to behavioral performance (Viswanathan et al., 2023). 620 621 Within this framework, the stable alpha-based RDM patterns from preparation through stimulus 622 presentation suggest that alpha oscillations persistently represent attentional state. We observe 623 robust separation of attentive versus passive conditions and spatial weighting of parietal-624 occipital channels, both consistent with models in which posterior alpha maintains the gain 625 control that prioritizes certain sensory inputs for subsequent processing. In our data, alpha thus 626 appears to index an ongoing control signal that maintains target-relevant representations over 627 time. At the same time, our scalp-level measurements cannot isolate which neural generators 628 are primarily responsible for the observed alpha patterns. Future work using measurements with 629 high spatial precision or causal perturbations (e.g., parietal or frontal TMS) will be necessary to 630 determine which nodes of the attention network actually generate the persistent alpha-band 631 representational structure we report here. 632 633

When we further 636 separated spatial attention by target location (i.e., left vs. right hemifield), alpha-based spatial 637 importance maps exhibited clear parietal lateralization, particularly 300–500 ms following cue 638 onset and 300 ms after the target (Figure 5 and 7). This lateralized pattern is consistent with 639 previous reports of alpha-band modulation during spatial attention (Deng et al., 2020; 640 Wöstmann et al., 2017; Gutteling et al., 2022). Together, these findings suggest that distributed 641 alpha-band dynamics carry information about both the spatial and non-spatial dimensions of 642 auditory attention, with spatial attention exhibiting a more distinct topography. 643 644 We note that alpha power can also be modulated by factors such as overall arousal and 645 listening effort (e.g., Obleser et al., 2012; Wöstmann et al., 2017). However, in the present study 646 the acoustic input in the Stimulus Period was identical across tasks, the different attentional 647 conditions were interleaved with relatively short intervals, and behavioral performance was high 648 in both tasks. These features make it unlikely that our alpha effects can be fully explained by 649 systematic differences in these factors between tasks. 650 651 The fact that alpha-based representations differentiate both the type of attentional cue (spatial 652 versus talker identity) and the specific spatial focus (left versus right) shows that alpha-related 653 processes do more than distinguish attentive from passive states; they also carry information 654 about which acoustic dimension is guiding attention. Many studies have linked alpha increases 655 to spatial attention in both auditory and visual tasks (Deng et al., 2020; Gutteling et al., 2022), 656 but only a few have examined how non-spatial attention (such as talker identity) is expressed in 657 alpha-band activity (Wöstmann et al., 2017; Viswanathan et al., 2023). Our findings therefore 658 extend prior work on spatial alpha by demonstrating that the distributed patterns of alpha-band 659 activity can distinguish spatial from non-spatial attentional sets such as talker-based attention. 660 661 Our results also indicate a limit to what can be decoded from EEG in this paradigm. Although 662 talker-based attention formed a distinct representational pattern in alpha-band activity, we could 663 not reliably discriminate between attention to female versus male talkers within the talker 664 attention task. This may reflect both the limited spatial resolution of EEG and the distributed 665 cortical representation of voice pitch and timbre (Burle et al., 2015; Hamilton et al., 2021). 666 Future work that contrasts spatial selection from multiple forms of non-spatial selection (e.g., 667 talker identity, pitch, temporal envelope, semantic category) at the representational level will be 668 important for determining how specifically alpha-related processes code spatial versus non-669 spatial attention. Broadband voltage showed strong representational strength at two 676 different time points, one near target cue onset and the other after the target syllable (Figure 8a 677 and 9a). In contrast, representations encoded in alpha power gradually increased after the cue 678 and remained elevated throughout the trial (Figure 8b and 9b). In particular, scalp voltage 679 exhibited representational dissimilarity for attentive-passive contrasts, in which key information 680 appears in a specific stimulus at a specific time. Conversely, alpha power encoded both 681 attentive-passive and spatial-talker attention contrasts, capturing sustained task engagement 682 and attentional state. Spatial importance maps also differed at these time points: broadband 683 voltage showed peaks over frontocentral channels, whereas alpha power was strongest over 684 parietal-occipital sensors. These differences remained after removing information shared 685 between the two features. 686 687 Compared to the complementary relationship broadband scalp voltage and alpha oscillations, 688 information in other frequency bands is largely redundant with these two signals (Supplemental 689 figures 4–7 and 9–12). Lower frequency bands (delta, theta) showed rapid increases in 690 discrimination accuracy during the Cue Period that faded quickly, likely reflecting ERP 691 components within those bands. Consistent with this, theta-band spatial importance maps 692 during the Cue Period resembled those of EEG scalp voltage. This suggests that, at least in this 693 paradigm, much of the discriminative information in delta and theta is redundant with broadband 694 evoked activity, rather than providing a window into any other attentional mechanisms. 695 696 Higher frequencies (beta, gamma) displayed patterns similar to those in alpha power but with 697 considerably weaker effects. Unique contribution analysis emphasized alpha power’s 698 dominance, revealing substantial overlap of the information conveyed by alpha and these other 699 bands. Prior research has suggested that gamma band fluctuations contribute to speech 700 processing, and are modulated by attention (Viswanathan et al., 2019; Synigal et al., 2020). 701 Consistent with this, we find that high frequency features do carry information distinguishing 702 attentive from passive listening. However, the small unique contribution of beta and gamma 703 power (despite their visual resemblance to alpha patterns) indicates that high-frequency induced 704 activity adds relatively little independent information about task state beyond what is already 705 captured by alpha in this task design. 706 707 Together, these results demonstrate that RSA-based unique contribution analysis can help 708 disentangle which EEG features truly add independent information about attentional state rather 709 than simply re-expressing the same underlying neural dynamics in a different EEG feature. 710 Limitations & Future work 711 We estimated dissimilarity between conditions using classifier accuracy rather than Euclidean, 712 crossnobis, or correlation-based distance measures (Schütt et al., 2023; see Diedrichsen & 713 Kriegeskorte, 2017 for review). Moreover, a multi-channel classifier yields weight maps that can 716 highlight the sensors or spatiotemporal patterns contributing most strongly to distinguishing 717 different states. We used a linear SVM as the decoding model because it combines moderate 718 performance with a simple linear decision rule: it implements a weighted summation followed by 719 a decision boundary, matching the standard linear readout assumptions often used in 720 neuroscience and making our assumptions about downstream decoding explicit (Hung et al., 721 2005; Yamins et al., 2014; Diedrichsen & Kriegeskorte, 2017). Using a linear SVM also ensures 722 that these weight maps arise from a simple linear relationship between features and classifier 723 output and can be transformed into interpretable encoding patterns. 724 725 At the same time, using classifier accuracy introduces several limitations. Dissimilarity estimates 726 depend on the choice of classifier, regularization, and cross  validation scheme, potentially 727 reducing robustness and reproducibility. In our study, we mitigated this by using a simple linear 728 model and validated regularization parameters, but the conceptual dependence remains. In 729 addition, although Haufe  transformed weight maps make it possible to move from classifier 730 weights to activation patterns, interpreting individual sensor values as positively or negatively 731 associated with an outcome remains difficult, since the weights could be influenced by noise 732 correlations and multicollinearity (Haufe et al., 2014). Future work should therefore complement 733 accuracy  based RDMs with continuous distance measures. 734 735 Intrinsic differences in temporal resolution between scalp voltage and frequency-band features 736 is an important limitation for our dynamic RSA. Although each RDM is computed on data from a 737 single timepoint, the covariance between successive timepoints differs between the broadband 738 voltage and the oscillatory power. Each estimate of power at a given time and frequency 739 requires integration over several cycles of the underlying oscillation, yielding temporal smearing 740 and uncertainty. These temporal correlations mean that decoding at one time point can be 741 influenced by activity occurring slightly before or after that specific time—and this temporal 742 dependence differs across EEG features. As a result, the temporal precision of our time-743 resolved RDMs differ across broadband scalp voltage and for frequency-band features (not to 744 mention across different frequency bands), limiting the validity of cross-feature comparisons. 745 746 This caveat means that fine-grained timing differences–especially when comparing scalp 747 voltage to band power–should be interpreted cautiously. Given these constraints, we focused 748 our interpretation on effects that exceeded the temporal precision of our measures: scalp-749 voltage discriminability typically unfolded over windows of roughly 100 ms or more, and alpha-750 based discriminability was sustained over several hundred milliseconds, both longer than the 751 temporal smearing introduced by preprocessing and time-frequency decomposition. Future work 752 could systematically manipulate and match the temporal smoothing applied to different EEG 753 features. 754 755 We trained classifiers for each feature independently for simplicity, but cross-feature interactions 756 may influence results (Patel et al., 2022; Studenova et al., 2023).
